Chapter 16

Curriculum Specification

The cognitive layer defines what to build. This document defines how to build it — translating the framework's capability model into concrete tasks, evaluation criteria, and progression constraints a teacher or institution can actually execute.

A framework that cannot be operationalized is philosophy. This specification bridges the gap between the cognitive layer's theory of sovereignty and the practical reality of teaching it to a child. It does not replace teacher judgment — it gives teacher judgment something real to act on.

How to Read This Document

The cognitive layer chapter specifies what cognitive sovereignty is and defines its exit condition. This document specifies how to develop it over time. The two documents work together: the chapter sets the requirements, this specification shows how to meet them.

The structure moves from general to specific. The capability architecture section defines the dependency order. The exit condition section defines the Grade 12 finish line. The grade band overview gives the longitudinal arc. The Grade 5 worked example is the most important section — it demonstrates how the general principles translate into concrete classroom practice, and it is the template for every other grade band.

Capability Architecture

The five capabilities defined in the cognitive layer are not parallel — they form a directed dependency structure. No capability can be meaningfully exercised without the ones it depends on. Instruction must follow this order; assessment must diagnose failures at the correct level.

  1. Logic and Formal Reasoning — the foundation. Evaluating argument structure independently of content. No other capability works without this.
  2. Evidence Evaluation — depends on logic. Assessing what evidence supports and how strongly. Requires the ability to distinguish valid inference from invalid inference before you can assess whether a conclusion follows from evidence.
  3. Adversarial Reasoning — depends on logic and evidence evaluation. Constructing the strongest case against your own position. Requires both correct reasoning about the opposing view and honest evaluation of its evidence.
  4. Philosophical and Ethical Reasoning — depends on all three. Engaging with questions that have no empirical answer. Requires the full analytical toolkit to avoid the question collapsing into either dogmatism or relativism.
  5. Confrontation with Ultimate Questions — requires the complete capability set. The hardest application, not an introduction. Students who reach this level are applying all prior capabilities simultaneously to the questions with the highest personal and existential stakes.
Diagnostic Principle

When a student fails at a higher capability, diagnose at the level below before intervening at the level of failure. A student who cannot steelman opposing positions (capability 3) may be missing evidence evaluation (capability 2), not adversarial reasoning specifically. Treating the symptom without addressing the root will not produce durable capability.

The Grade 12 Exit Condition

A student exits the curriculum as cognitively sovereign if they can demonstrate all of the following under conditions designed to stress each capability:

Structural analysis. Given an argument they have not encountered before, in a domain they have not studied, they can identify its logical structure, name any fallacies present, and evaluate whether the conclusion follows from the premises — independent of whether they agree with the conclusion.

Evidence assessment. Given a novel claim supported by evidence, they can assess the quality of the evidence (sample size, methodology, source reliability, alternative explanations), state what the evidence does and does not support, and identify what additional evidence would change their assessment.

Steelmanning under pressure. Given a position they disagree with, in a setting where peers and facilitators favor their own position, they can construct the strongest possible version of the opposing argument — one that a proponent would endorse — and identify the conditions under which that argument would be correct.

Philosophical engagement. Given a genuine ethical dilemma with no empirical resolution, they can identify the competing frameworks that apply, the assumptions each framework depends on, and the costs and benefits of each — without collapsing into "it's just a matter of opinion" or asserting certainty they cannot justify.

Belief revision. Given new evidence or a better argument, they can update their position, explain specifically what changed and why, and do this without the revision being driven by social pressure rather than epistemic grounds.

Self-application. They can apply all of the above to their own beliefs — including beliefs formed in the context of the curriculum itself. They can identify assumptions they hold that they cannot fully justify, name the arguments against their own worldview, and hold genuine uncertainty without paralysis.

What the Exit Condition Is Not

The exit condition does not require students to hold any particular beliefs, reach any particular conclusions, or reject any particular tradition. A student can be a committed Christian, a committed atheist, a committed socialist, or a committed libertarian and fully meet the exit condition — as long as they can demonstrate that their commitments have survived genuine scrutiny and that they hold them with appropriate understanding of what it would take to change their mind. The exit condition tests the faculty of judgment, not its outputs.

Grade Band Overview

Capabilities are introduced sequentially and reinforced cumulatively. Each grade band targets the capability appropriate to developmental stage while continuing to exercise capabilities introduced in earlier bands.

Grade Band Primary Capability Core Skill Being Built
PreK–2 Logic (foundation) Distinguishing valid from invalid inference in concrete, familiar situations. "If all dogs have tails and Max is a dog, does Max have a tail?"
Grades 3–4 Logic (applied) Identifying fallacies in simple arguments: circular reasoning, appeal to authority, false dichotomy. Applied to everyday disagreements and simple persuasion.
Grades 5–6 Evidence Evaluation Assessing source quality, distinguishing correlation from causation, identifying what evidence does and does not support. Logic continues as a tool.
Grades 7–8 Adversarial Reasoning (intro) Constructing the opposing argument. Structured steelmanning. Learning to distinguish the best version of a position from the worst version.
Grades 9–10 Adversarial Reasoning (applied) Steelmanning under social pressure. Inoculation exercises: exposure to sophisticated propaganda and persuasion with active deconstruction. Belief revision under evidence.
Grades 11–12 Philosophical & Ultimate Questions Full integration. Genuine engagement with ethics, metaphysics, and worldview. Self-application of all prior capabilities to student's own commitments. Exit condition assessment.

Grade 5 — Worked Example

Grade 5 is the entry point for evidence evaluation. Students at this level have two years of applied logic training behind them. They can identify simple fallacies and evaluate argument structure in familiar domains. The Grade 5 curriculum extends this foundation into the question of evidence: not just does the argument follow, but what does the evidence actually support?

Capabilities Targeted

  • Primary: Evidence evaluation — source reliability, distinguishing what evidence supports from what it does not, identifying missing evidence
  • Continued: Logic — fallacy identification, argument structure analysis (these are exercised in every task, not standalone lessons)
  • Introduced: Basic adversarial framing — "what would someone who disagrees with this say?" (preparation for Grade 7–8 steelmanning)

Sample Tasks with Evaluation Criteria

Task 1 — Source Reliability

Prompt: "A company that sells sunscreen publishes a study showing that sunscreen prevents skin cancer. A university hospital publishes a study with the same finding. Which study gives you more reason to believe the claim? Why?"

Pass: Student identifies that the company has a financial interest in the result, explains that this creates a reason to doubt the result independent of the study's methodology, and names the hospital study as providing stronger evidence because the source has less incentive to produce a particular result. Student may note that both could be wrong, or that the methodology of each matters too.

Fail: Student picks the more authoritative-sounding source without explaining why. Student says both are equally valid without engaging with the conflict of interest. Student dismisses one source entirely without engaging with what the study actually shows. Student appeals to authority ("doctors know more") without addressing the incentive structure.

Failure mode to watch for: Students often recognize that bias exists but treat it as disqualifying rather than as a degree of evidence. The goal is calibration, not binary credibility assignment.

Task 2 — What Evidence Supports

Prompt: "A study of 200 students found that students who ate breakfast scored higher on tests than students who didn't. A school decides to require breakfast for all students to improve test scores. Is this a good decision based on this evidence? What's missing?"

Pass: Student identifies at least two of the following: (a) correlation does not establish that breakfast causes higher scores — students who eat breakfast may differ from those who don't in other ways; (b) the sample is small; (c) we don't know if requiring breakfast changes behavior or just shifts who eats; (d) there may be confounding variables (income, home stability). Student identifies what additional evidence would strengthen the case (a controlled experiment, a larger sample, data on whether requiring breakfast actually increases consumption).

Fail: Student accepts the conclusion because the evidence "supports" it without examining what the correlation actually establishes. Student rejects the conclusion entirely without engaging with what the evidence does show. Student cannot articulate what additional evidence would help.

Failure mode to watch for: Students at this age often oscillate between "evidence proves it" and "evidence means nothing." The skill being built is proportional confidence — the conclusion is possible, perhaps likely, but not established.

Task 3 — Novel Domain (Anti-Gaming Check)

Prompt: [Present a short, unfamiliar scenario — e.g., a fictitious historical event or a made-up scientific claim about a domain students have not studied.] "A historian writes that the Kelmarion Empire collapsed because of trade route disruption. The historian's evidence is that trade records declined in the 50 years before the collapse. Does this evidence prove the claim? What else would you want to know?"

Pass: Student applies the same reasoning as in familiar domains — identifies that declining trade records are consistent with the claim but do not prove it, asks about alternative causes, asks whether there were other empires that experienced trade decline without collapse, identifies what additional evidence would establish the connection more firmly.

Fail: Student cannot transfer the reasoning to an unfamiliar domain. Student treats unfamiliarity as a reason to accept the expert's authority rather than a reason to apply the evidence evaluation toolkit. Student is unable to identify what questions to ask.

Failure mode to watch for: This task specifically detects whether students have acquired a capability or memorized a pattern. Students who fail here may perform well on Tasks 1 and 2 through pattern recognition. The novel domain strips that away.

Task 4 — Adversarial Introduction

Prompt: "You believe that homework helps students learn. What would someone who disagrees say? What evidence might they use? Is any of that evidence good?"

Pass: Student generates a genuine opposing argument — not a strawman. Student can identify evidence that might support the opposing view (studies showing diminishing returns, research on student stress, comparison of high-performing school systems that assign less homework). Student evaluates whether the opposing evidence is strong or weak without dismissing it because it conflicts with their view.

Fail: Student produces a weak version of the opposing argument that is easy to refute. Student cannot identify any evidence for the opposing view. Student identifies opposing evidence but immediately dismisses it without engagement. Student changes their own position to avoid conflict rather than genuinely evaluating the opposition.

Failure mode to watch for: Students often produce a version of the opposing argument calibrated to be easily defeated. This is the most important failure mode at this stage because it is the seed of sophisticated rationalization — constructing a "fair hearing" that is actually a trap. Ask students: "Would someone who disagrees agree that this is their best argument?"

Training Loop

Each of the four task types above requires a training loop, not a single exposure. The structure for each:

  1. Introduce the concept with a clear example where the distinction is obvious. Students should understand what they are being asked to notice before encountering cases where it is ambiguous.
  2. Practice with familiar domains where students have enough background knowledge to engage with the evidence itself. Two to three sessions.
  3. Practice with unfamiliar domains where students cannot rely on prior knowledge and must apply the capability procedurally. This is where the capability becomes transferable. Two to three sessions.
  4. Practice under mild social pressure — structured small-group exercises where students must defend an evidence evaluation against peers who disagree. The goal at Grade 5 is not full adversarial pressure (that is Grade 9–10) but the introduction of social friction. Students should experience maintaining a position when someone pushes back and then learn to distinguish "I held my position under pressure" from "I revised my position under evidence."
  5. Assess with novel domain (Task 3 structure). If a student passes the novel domain task, the capability is genuinely acquired. If they fail, return to step 3.

Failure Mode Summary for Grade 5

  • Binary credibility assignment: treating sources as fully credible or fully discredited rather than calibrating trust by incentive structure and methodology
  • Correlation acceptance: treating correlation as sufficient evidence for causation without requiring experimental design or controlled comparison
  • Domain lock: applying evidence evaluation correctly in practiced domains but failing to transfer to novel ones — the signal that a pattern has been memorized rather than a capability acquired
  • Strawman steelmanning: constructing a weak version of the opposing argument and believing this constitutes adversarial reasoning
  • Proportionality collapse: either treating any evidence as proving a conclusion or treating any uncertainty as disproving it — the inability to hold calibrated, partial confidence

Evaluation Principles

The evaluation model for this curriculum differs from standard assessment in three ways that matter.

Process over conclusion. The correct answer is not a belief — it is a reasoning process. Two students who reach opposite conclusions on an ambiguous evidence task can both receive full marks if both demonstrate appropriate calibration, correct identification of what the evidence supports, and genuine engagement with what the opposing view would say. Two students who reach the same conclusion can both fail if they reached it through pattern-matching rather than reasoning.

Novel domain requirement. No assessment task may use a domain or scenario the student has been exposed to in instruction. Recognizing a familiar pattern is not evidence of a capability. The evaluation infrastructure must include a bank of genuinely novel scenarios maintained separately from instruction materials.

Pressure testing. For capabilities 3–5 (adversarial reasoning and above), assessment must include social pressure conditions. Written assessments alone cannot evaluate whether a student can maintain a reasoned position when someone pushes back. This requires structured oral or group assessment components.

Anti-Gaming Safeguards

These are structural constraints on the curriculum and assessment design, not individual teacher interventions. They operate at the institutional level.

Scenario separation. Instruction scenarios and assessment scenarios must be maintained in separate banks. Instructors draw assessment tasks from the assessment bank; they do not create assessments from their own teaching materials. This prevents students from learning the "right" reasoning about specific cases rather than acquiring the underlying capability.

Process documentation. Students are required to document their reasoning process, not just their conclusions. Assessors evaluate the documented process against the pass/fail criteria, not the conclusion. This makes it possible to distinguish correct conclusions reached by flawed reasoning from incorrect conclusions reached by sound reasoning applied to incomplete evidence.

Facilitator training requirement. The adversarial reasoning and inoculation components (Grades 9–10) require facilitators trained specifically in the failure modes. Poorly facilitated adversarial reasoning exercises produce cynicism — the belief that all reasoning is rationalization — rather than genuine adversarial capacity. Facilitator certification for these components is non-negotiable. A school that cannot staff these sessions correctly should not run them at all.

Curriculum self-audit. The curriculum is subject to the same adversarial analysis it asks of students. Each revision cycle should include a structured exercise in which educators apply the full toolkit to the curriculum itself: What assumptions does it make? What is the strongest case that it produces a new form of indoctrination rather than genuine reasoning? What evidence would indicate this is happening? This is not performative — it is the mechanism by which the curriculum remains honest about its own limitations.

Further Development

This specification provides the full worked example for Grade 5 and the evaluation and anti-gaming frameworks that apply across all grade bands. The remaining grade band worked examples (PreK–2, Grades 3–4, 7–8, 9–10, 11–12) follow the same structure as the Grade 5 example: target capabilities, sample tasks with pass/fail criteria, failure mode summary, and training loop. Development of those examples should follow from pilot implementation of the Grade 5 model — the test of whether this specification is correct is whether it can be executed, and the Grade 5 band is the right starting point for that test.
