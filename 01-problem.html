Chapter 01

The Problem of Dependency

The current international order does not operate on cooperation. It operates on control — exercised primarily through the management of dependency relationships that most nations cannot escape.

Since the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, the implicit rule of the international system has been that power correlates with size — territory, population, industrial base, military capacity. Small countries survive by aligning with large ones, trading autonomy for protection. This arrangement is presented as cooperation. It is, in structural terms, a dependency relationship that can be revoked.

Dependency as Control

The mechanisms of dependency are varied, but they share a common logic: a nation that cannot provide for itself in a critical domain is subject to the will of whoever controls that domain. The instruments are familiar.

Energy dependency means that a nation's economy, its heating systems, its transportation, and its military readiness are all contingent on the decisions of energy-exporting nations. Russia's gas leverage over Europe in the wake of the Ukraine conflict demonstrated this with painful clarity. Nations that believed they had diversified energy partnerships discovered that dependency on a single critical input — in this case, natural gas — gave a hostile actor veto power over their foreign policy.

Financial dependency operates through the global dollar system, the SWIFT payment network, and the institutional architecture of international lending. A nation that cannot transact without access to systems controlled by another nation is not sovereign in any meaningful sense. The freezing of Russia's foreign reserves in 2022 demonstrated that even a major power's accumulated wealth exists only at the discretion of the nations that control the financial infrastructure.

Technology dependency is perhaps the most consequential and least visible form of control. A nation that runs its government, military, communications, and economy on hardware and software designed and manufactured by other nations has embedded foreign decision-making into its most critical systems. The dependence on Taiwanese semiconductor fabrication, American operating systems, and Chinese rare earth processing means that most nations' technological infrastructure is, in structural terms, a foreign concession that can be withdrawn.

Food dependency transforms a biological necessity into a geopolitical lever. Nations that import significant portions of their caloric intake are subject to supply chain disruptions, price manipulation, and outright embargo. The global grain crisis following the Ukraine conflict demonstrated that food-importing nations — particularly in the Middle East and North Africa — faced not just economic hardship but political destabilization as a direct consequence of their dependency on distant agricultural production.

Cognitive dependency is the newest and most insidious form. A nation whose population consumes information through platforms controlled by foreign entities — social media algorithms designed in Silicon Valley, news ecosystems shaped by external editorial decisions, educational content produced by foreign institutions — has outsourced the formation of its citizens' reasoning to actors with no obligation to that nation's interests. This is not a conspiracy. It is a structural condition created by the concentration of information infrastructure in a small number of countries.

The Dependency Trap

The most significant feature of dependency is that it is self-reinforcing. A nation that depends on imported energy cannot easily develop domestic energy because the capital and technology required for that development are themselves controlled by the nations it depends on. A nation that depends on foreign technology cannot develop its own technology base because the educational and industrial infrastructure required for technological sovereignty requires decades of investment that the dependent nation's economic structure — shaped by its dependency — cannot sustain.

This creates a stable equilibrium that serves the interests of the nations at the top of the dependency hierarchy and permanently constrains the nations at the bottom. The system is not maintained by force — though force is available as a backstop. It is maintained by the structure of dependency itself, which makes independence appear irrational to any individual actor operating within the system's logic.

Core Observation

The international order does not prevent nations from achieving independence. It makes independence appear economically irrational by structuring global markets, financial systems, and technology supply chains so that dependency is always the cheaper short-term option. The Sovereign Stack is designed to make independence the rational long-term strategy by addressing every layer of dependency simultaneously.

The Hierarchy of Dependence

Not all nations occupy the same position in the dependency structure. A rough hierarchy exists.

At the top are multi-domain sovereign powers — nations that are independent or near-independent across most critical domains. Today this category arguably includes only the United States (with dependencies on rare earth minerals and pharmaceutical precursors) and, to a lesser degree, China (with dependencies on food imports, energy imports, and continued dollar-system access). Even these powers are not fully sovereign.

Domain-specific powers occupy the middle tier — nations sovereign in one or two critical domains but dependent in others. Russia is energy-sovereign and militarily capable but technologically dependent. Saudi Arabia is energy-sovereign but dependent in food, technology, and institutional capacity. Germany is institutionally sophisticated but energy-dependent and militarily constrained.

At the bottom are structurally dependent nations — countries that lack sovereignty in every critical domain and survive through alignment with one or more higher-tier powers. This category includes most of the world's nations. Their sovereignty is nominal. Their actual autonomy extends only to those decisions that do not conflict with the interests of the nations they depend on.

Why This Matters Now

The dependency structure of the international order has been relatively stable for decades, managed by institutions — the UN, the IMF, the World Bank, the WTO — that were designed to maintain the system rather than transform it. But several converging pressures are destabilizing this equilibrium.

Great power competition between the United States and China is forcing nations to choose between dependency systems rather than diversifying across them. The era of strategic ambiguity — maintaining economic ties with China while relying on American security guarantees — is closing. Nations that cannot achieve sovereignty in critical domains will be forced into one sphere or the other, with diminishing ability to negotiate terms.

Climate change is redistributing the physical inputs that underpin sovereignty. Agricultural zones are shifting. Water systems are destabilizing. Energy demands are changing. Nations whose sovereignty strategies were calibrated to twentieth-century geography will find those strategies inadequate for twenty-first-century conditions.

Artificial intelligence is concentrating cognitive and technological power in a smaller number of actors than at any point in human history. A nation that does not develop domestic AI capability will find its economic, military, and information systems shaped by AI systems designed elsewhere, for purposes defined elsewhere, with priorities set elsewhere. This is the technological dependency problem accelerated by orders of magnitude.

The weaponization of interdependence has accelerated. Sanctions, export controls, financial system exclusion, and technology denial are no longer exceptional measures. They are routine instruments of statecraft. Every dependency relationship that was once framed as mutual benefit is now a potential vector of coercion. Nations are recognizing this, but recognition without a comprehensive framework for achieving independence produces only anxiety, not solutions.

The Opportunity

The same technologies that are concentrating power — renewable energy, AI, cryptographic systems, regenerative agriculture, distributed manufacturing — also make it possible, for the first time in history, for small and mid-sized nations to achieve genuine sovereignty across multiple domains. The Sovereign Stack is a framework for seizing that opportunity before the window closes.

What Sovereignty Actually Means

Sovereignty in this framework is not autarky — complete self-sufficiency with no external engagement. Autarky is neither achievable nor desirable. It produces isolation, stagnation, and ultimately weakness.

Sovereignty is the condition in which a nation's critical systems cannot be disrupted, denied, or manipulated by external actors as a means of coercion. A sovereign nation may choose to trade, cooperate, and engage with the global system. The key word is choose. A sovereign nation's participation in international systems is voluntary and can be withdrawn without existential consequence. A dependent nation's participation is compulsory — withdrawal means crisis.

The Sovereign Stack identifies every domain in which dependency creates vulnerability, and provides an architecture for achieving independence — or sufficient resilience — in each one. The result is not a nation that refuses to engage with the world. It is a nation that engages from a position of genuine freedom rather than structural compulsion.

The chapters that follow describe that architecture, layer by layer, in the sequence that matters.
